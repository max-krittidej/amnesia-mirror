  \item \field{Innovation Concept and Background}

    \textbf{The problem.} A person cannot hold a job if they cannot reliably manage
    the ordinary structure of a day: taking medication, remembering appointments, and
    following task steps in order. The target user is someone who may understand a
    task when supported, but forgets what to do, when to do it, or what step comes
    next when alone. In Thailand, only
    \textbf{36.3\%} of roughly 855{,}000 working-age people with disability are
    employed, and only \textbf{8\%} reach the competitive labour market~[3, 4].
    Intellectual disability (about 145{,}000 registered) and mental or behavioural
    disability (about 167{,}000) make this a large, under-served group~[2].

    \textbf{Target group.} Working-age adults (15--59) with cognitive or memory
    disability: intellectual disability, traumatic brain injury, post-stroke
    cognitive impairment, or early-onset dementia.

    \textbf{Why current solutions fall short.} The disability allowance is income
    support, not a route to work~[1], and employers often pay quota fines rather than
    hire~[5]. Existing cognitive aids are mostly phone- or tablet-based, asking users
    to remember, carry, charge, unlock, and navigate the very device that is meant to
    compensate for memory problems. Voice assistants such as Alexa or Google Home can
    set reminders, but they are not designed around a user's visual context, caregiver
    authored routines, or gradual prompt-fading. A memory aid should not itself demand
    memory, and a reminder should know what situation the person is actually in.

    \textbf{Why an ambient smart mirror, and why now.} A mirror is fixed furniture in
    the user's daily path. It needs no activation ritual, does not look medical, and
    can prompt at the exact moment of use: medication, orientation, or a task step.
    This is feasible now: smart-mirror cognitive support and independent-living mirrors
    have been demonstrated~[6, 7], and prompting technology has evidence for reducing
    assistance in daily activities~[8].

    \textbf{From learning to earning.} Reliable routines create independence;
    independence enables practice; practice supports employability. MindMirror carries
    the same step-by-step prompting from daily living into work skills, turning
    assistive technology's proven employment potential~[10] into a concrete path from
    learning to earning.
