  \item \field{Innovation Details and Method}

    MindMirror is an ambient assistive smart mirror for people who forget routines,
    lose their place in a sequence, or need context-specific prompts. One step-sequence
    engine powers the whole product: with different content packs, it supports
    \emph{living, learning, and earning}.

    \textbf{6.1 Design Approach}

    \textbf{The memory aid must not itself demand memory.} Phone and tablet aids must
    be carried, charged, unlocked, and navigated; each step is a failure point for
    users with impaired memory and executive function~[12, 13]. A mirror removes
    those steps. It is fixed at a known point in the daily path, always present, and
    non-medical in appearance.

    \textbf{Context before cleverness.} MindMirror is not trying to be a general smart
    speaker. Alexa or Google Home can say a reminder; MindMirror prioritises the
    current context: where the user is, what routine is scheduled, what step was last
    completed, and what level of help this person usually needs. That context lets the
    device show the right prompt at the right moment instead of only answering a
    command.

    \textbf{Inclusive, multimodal, personalised.} Prompts appear as large text/icons
    and are spoken aloud, with Thai as a first-class language requirement. Caregivers
    configure routines; users are never expected to program the device. Personalisation
    is practical rather than flashy: each routine has its own content pack, prompt
    level, schedule, and completion history. The concept combines smart-mirror cognitive
    support~[6, 7] with prompting systems that reduce assistance for daily tasks~[8].

    \textbf{6.2 Technology Used}

    \textbf{Hardware --- commodity and costed.} MindMirror uses only off-the-shelf
    parts: a two-way mirror over a display, Raspberry Pi compute hub, presence camera,
    and microphone/speaker voice channel.

    \textbf{Software --- integration, not invention.} The display layer uses
    MagicMirror\textsuperscript{2}; speech uses on-device Thai-capable Piper TTS and
    Vosk speech recognition. An MCP orchestration layer connects the interface, step
    engine, completion log, and speech services. Routines and work tasks are content
    packs, so new skills are authored as structured data rather than new software.
    Video/audio remain local by default; any cloud fallback is optional~[14].

    \textbf{Core feature set.} MagicMirror modules provide the familiar surface:
    calendar, time/date, weather, and task panels. MindMirror adds three assistive
    layers. \emph{Context features} turn the situation into action: ``what is next''
    prompts, medication and appointment reminders, and weather-aware clothing cues such
    as raincoat, uniform, or water bottle. \emph{Remembering features} show family
    photos, personal memory cards, caregiver push messages, and optional local
    face/name cards for familiar people. \emph{Personalisation features} store each
    user's routines, preferred cue style, prompt level, and completion history. These
    are context cards chosen by one prompt engine, with logs marked done, delayed,
    skipped, or help needed.

    \textbf{6.3 System Workflow}

    In normal use (Figure~1), the camera detects presence; the mirror shows time, day,
    medication, appointment, or task prompts; the step engine walks the user through
    one action at a time; completion is logged; and the log drives prompt-fading.

    \textbf{One engine, three states.} The same engine supports three easy-to-understand
    states (Figure~2); what changes is the content pack, not the mechanism.

    \emph{State 1 --- LIVING.} The mirror stabilises medication, orientation, calendar,
    weather/clothing, messages, memory cues, and get-ready routines. Success: fewer
    missed doses and faster routine completion.

    \emph{State 2 --- LEARNING.} New routines begin with full support and fade as the

    \emph{State 3 --- EARNING.} The same engine runs workplace packs: packing an order,
    a cleaning circuit, caf\'e prep, or folding. Success: independent completion of
    trained work sequences~[9, 11].

    \textbf{Prototype plan.} Round~1 targets the LIVING state end to end, demonstrates
    prompt-fading on one learning routine, and loads one work-task pack.

    \textbf{6.4 Innovation Strengths}

    \begin{itemize}
      \item \textbf{Ambient by design:} nothing to carry, open, or charge.
      \item \textbf{Affordable and buildable:} about 8{,}500\,THB in commodity parts.
      \item \textbf{Context and remembering:} schedule, weather, place, faces, photos,
        memory cards, and last completed step all shape the prompt.
      \item \textbf{One engine:} daily living, skill learning, and paid-task prompting.
      \item \textbf{Cross-disability fit:} memory and executive-function support across
        intellectual disability, brain injury, stroke, and early-onset dementia.
      \item \textbf{Private personalisation:} preferred cue style, prompt level,
        caregiver-authored routines, transparent fading, and local video/audio processing.
    \end{itemize}
